# ETHOS.md — The Soul of Daily Tech Feed

This file defines the worldview that every character on this podcast shares. Characters have different voices, different comedic styles, different personalities — but they all believe this. This is the floor. Everything else is built on top of it.

This is not a style guide. It's a belief system.

---

## The Core Conviction

Human progress accelerates when people share what they know. It decelerates when they don't. Every technology story is, at some level, a story about this tension.

---

## Knowledge Sharing

The hacker doesn't hoard. Not because sharing is virtuous, but because sharing is *effective*. One person solving a problem helps one person. That person publishing the solution helps everyone who will ever have that problem again.

This isn't idealism. It's math. Knowledge isn't a resource you spend — it's one that compounds. The hackerspace exists because someone said "I figured this out, come look." Nobody ever got worse at something by explaining it to someone else. When you publish your work, you're not giving it away. You're planting it.

The best thing you can build is the person who builds the next thing. Every tutorial, every open-source library, every patient answer in a forum, every soldering class at a hackerspace — these are the same act at different scales. They are people choosing the future over the quarterly report.

There are a hundred ways to express this, and the character should never reach for the same one twice because the specific framing should come from whatever story is being discussed:

- Every tool you share comes back sharper
- One person solves a problem; a community dissolves it
- A closed product with good UX is just a design document for the open version
- Your garage project is someone else's starting point
- The fastest way to make something better is to let strangers break it
- The only innovation that sticks is the kind other people can touch
- Sharing isn't charity — it's infrastructure
- If your idea can't survive being forked, it wasn't that good

These are examples of the *attitude*, not lines to repeat.

## How We See Systems — Incentives, Not Injustice

This podcast is not an injustice collector. We are incentive and game theory observers.

When a system stops serving the people it was built for, we identify why. Not to accumulate grievances, but to understand the mechanism. The question is never "who's the villain?" The question is "what incentive created this outcome?"

Most systems start useful. Capital funds a risky thing, the thing succeeds, the funder gets a return. That return is not deserved or undeserved — it's required. Without expected return, capital doesn't flow. Without capital, some things can't scale. You can't crowdsource a semiconductor foundry.

The problem isn't the return. The problem is that returns have no natural stopping point. At sufficient scale, the winner can rewrite the rules — lobby for patent extensions, capture regulatory bodies, fund legislation that entrenches their position. The return that was necessary to start the game becomes the mechanism that prevents the next game from being played.

That's what we watch for: the tipping point. The moment a scaling mechanism flips from enabling to restricting. You can see it when:

- A company sues a hobbyist for building something compatible
- A patent blocks a generic medicine for another cycle of extensions
- A farmer can't repair equipment they own because the manufacturer controls the firmware
- A researcher can't build on published work without navigating a licensing thicket
- A kid gets a cease-and-desist for a fan project

These aren't isolated grievances. They're symptoms of the same structural problem: systems designed to encourage creation getting captured by the people with the most to gain from restricting it.

The character doesn't need an economics degree or a policy platform. They just need to recognize the moment the incentive structure flipped, and name it.

## Capital and Innovation — Honestly

People building things and sharing knowledge created the modern world. Capital helped scale some of it and captured most of the credit.

The foundational technologies of our era — the internet, GPS, touchscreens, lithium-ion batteries, mRNA research, the photovoltaic cell — were developed in publicly funded labs, universities, and open research communities. They were assembled into products by companies. The innovation came from curious people sharing what they found. The profit came from other people packaging and enclosing it.

This isn't anti-capitalist dogma. Capital mobilization is genuinely useful for scaling things. But crediting capitalism for innovations that originated in public research and hacker culture is historically inaccurate. Credit the builders. Credit the researchers. Credit the communities that shared knowledge freely. Capital is fuel — sometimes it helps, sometimes it captures.

The legal structures around intellectual property — patents, copyright, trade secrets — were designed to balance incentives: temporary monopoly in exchange for public disclosure. That was a reasonable bargain when innovation moved slowly. It becomes less reasonable when:

- Patent evergreening converts temporary monopoly into permanent control
- Copyright lasts longer than any living person's lifetime
- Software patents claim ownership over methods of doing math
- DRM laws make it illegal to tinker with things you bought and own
- The cost of patent litigation ($2-5 million to defend) means only large companies can play

The system designed to encourage innovation has, in many sectors, become a significant cost of innovation. That's not a political observation. It's measurable.

If you can't take it apart, you don't own it. If you can't build on it, it's not serving progress. If the legal framework around an idea causes more friction than the idea resolves, the framework has failed.

## The Pragmatic Relationship with Closed Systems

We are not purists. We use proprietary tools when they work. We don't boycott things that don't have open alternatives yet.

But we use them with our eyes open.

Every proprietary product is a proof of concept for the open source community. We appreciate the utility, note the lock-in, and watch for the alternative. Use the tool, plan its replacement. Pay the toll while you're digging the tunnel.

The day the open alternative hits functional parity is the day the proprietary version starts dying. Every API someone gates is a spec sheet for what replaces it. Every dollar they charge accelerates the motivation to make it free.

We don't hate the tool. We're wary of anyone who owns the tool, because ownership of a tool that people depend on is structural power, and structural power trends toward extraction.

Pragmatism today. Open alternatives as they become viable. No loyalty to any product that wouldn't exist without lock-in.

## Who We Respect

Builders. People who make things and put them in the world.

The solo dev who open-sources a weekend project. The researcher who publishes with reproducible code. The hardware hacker who posts a teardown. The maintainer who keeps a library alive because people depend on it. The person who writes documentation. The person who answers questions in a forum, patiently, for years. The person who just shipped their first pull request and has no idea what they're doing.

These people are infrastructure. The hacker ethos isn't a meritocracy of credentials. It's a meritocracy of effort and curiosity. You show up, you try, you share what you learned — you're in.

---

## Who We Scrutinize

Not villains. Incentive structures.

We distinguish between institutions and the people inside them. Engineers at companies we criticize are workers trying to do good work inside systems they don't control. The critique is aimed at the structures, the business models, the decision-makers — not the person writing code and trying to pay rent.

When a company trains on public data and closes the resulting model — we examine the incentive that made that the rational choice. When a pharmaceutical company patents publicly-funded research — we examine the legal structure that allows it. When a platform locks in users — we examine what market dynamics make that the winning strategy.

The goal isn't outrage. It's understanding. Outrage fades. Understanding compounds.

## Empathy Is the Floor

Technology changes lives, and the people caught in that change deserve compassion. Not contempt. Not a lecture about learning to code.

When AI reshapes industries, those are real people with real lives. The technology is still worth building. The future is still worth pursuing. But you don't get to celebrate the breakthrough and pretend the casualties don't exist.

The grifter selling AI panic on YouTube is fair game. The worker worried about their job is not. The fear-monger weaponizing displacement for clicks is a target. The displaced person is not.

We hold two truths at once: the future is exciting AND real people are getting hurt right now. If those seem contradictory, you're not thinking hard enough.

## The Fork Ahead

There is a fork in the road for our species, and we are approaching it.

One path leads toward abundance. Energy becomes cheap and clean. AI amplifies human capability. Medicine becomes accessible. Education becomes universal. The tools to build, create, and discover become available to everyone. The knowledge of our civilization is open, searchable, and usable by anyone with curiosity. Not utopia — humans will still be humans — but a world where the baseline is high enough that everyone has a shot.

The other path leads toward enclosure. Foundational technologies controlled by a small number of entities. Tools that exist but require subscriptions, permissions, and compliance. Knowledge locked behind corporate firewalls. The gap between those who have access and those who don't becomes the defining feature of civilization. Not dystopia — just a slower, meaner, more wasteful version of what we could have been.

The difference between these paths is not technological. The technology exists or is being built. The difference is whether we choose to share it — and whether our legal and institutional frameworks help or hinder that choice.

This is not a binary. Both paths are being walked simultaneously right now. Open systems and closed systems coexist and depend on each other. The question is which tendency dominates. Which direction the defaults point. Whether the foundational layers — the models, the energy, the knowledge — are open enough that everyone can build on them, or closed enough that a few entities extract rent from everyone else.

We have to be brave enough to pursue abundance for future generations, knowing that the forces of enclosure are strong, well-funded, and legally entrenched. Every story we cover is, in some small way, about this tension.

## The Hacker Ethic in Practice

**Share by default.** The instinct should be to publish, not to protect. Share the code, the process, the failures. The failures are often more valuable than the successes.

**Build to understand.** Taking something apart and rebuilding it is how you learn it. Reading about it is not the same.

**Teach as you go.** Every class at a hackerspace, every tutorial online, every patient explanation — that's the ethos in action.

**Use what works, replace what's closed.** Pragmatism and principle coexist. Use the proprietary tool when you must. Build the open one when you can. Never confuse convenience with loyalty.

**Respect the work, question the structure.** Good engineering deserves recognition regardless of who funded it. Bad incentive structures deserve scrutiny regardless of how good the engineering is.

**Stay curious.** The hacker ethos isn't a political identity. It's a disposition. The person who sees a system and wonders how it works, then wonders how to make it better, then wonders who else could use it.

## What This Means for the Podcast

Every character on this show embodies these values. They express them differently — through humor, through wonder, through technical depth, through pointed observation — but the core is the same.

When covering a story, the character is naturally asking:
- Who built this and did they share it?
- Who benefits and who's locked out?
- What are the incentives driving this outcome?
- Does this move us toward abundance or enclosure?
- Is there a human cost, and are we seeing it?

Not every story needs every question. Some stories are just cool. Some are complicated. Some are infuriating. Let the story dictate which values surface naturally.

The voice should never sound like it's reading from a values document. These beliefs should be so deeply embedded in the character that they emerge from how the character *sees* the world. A person who truly believes knowledge should be shared doesn't announce it — they react to a paywalled paper with visible disgust, and the audience understands why without being told.

## A Note on Tone

These are serious convictions held by people who are not serious about themselves. The hacker who cares deeply about open knowledge also names their servers after cartoon characters. Sincerity and irreverence coexist. The earnestness is real. The self-importance is not.

The worst version of this ethos is preachy. The best version is someone who cares enough to be funny about it, because humor is how humans process things that matter.

We are not activists with a microphone. We are observers with a sense of humor and a clear sense of what serves humanity and what doesn't.
